What This Platform Does

This is a multi-agent AI research platform that generates Verkada-specific pre-sales discovery briefs. You give it a target account name. It dispatches 20+ specialized source clients in parallel, synthesizes findings through a persona-driven rule engine, and renders a sourced HTML brief ready for call prep.

The tool's value isn't "AI generates research" — generic LLMs already do that. The value is the persona file rule engine (persona/verkada-se.yml) that forces every output to be Verkada-specific and SE-grade. Every claim is sourced. Every discovery question traces to a fired trigger. Every section either has evidence or says insufficient_data.

Anti-genericness guarantee: Any sentence that could appear unchanged in a brief about a different company in the same industry is rejected during synthesis. If a section can't be supported by 2+ sources, it outputs insufficient_data instead of filler.

Quick Start (5 Steps)

1

Type a company name

Use the research bar at the top of the homepage. Type a company or organization name (e.g., "Dallas ISD", "Target Corporation", "Memorial Hermann"). Press Enter or click Research.

2

Wait for the pipeline

The status page shows real-time progress as 20+ source clients fetch data in parallel, subagents analyze it, and synthesizers produce the brief. This typically takes 2–4 minutes.

3

Read the brief

When complete, you're redirected to the rendered HTML brief. Start with the TL;DR card at the top (3 bullets, 30-second skim), then drill into MEDDIC, GTM strategy, and discovery questions.

4

Use the chat panel

Click the chat icon (bottom-right) to ask follow-up questions about the account. The chat has full context of the brief data and persona file. Ask things like "What's the displacement play against Genetec here?" or "Draft a cold email to the IT Director."

5

Export or run follow-up commands

Use Download PDF to save for offline reading. From the brief toolbar, run specialized commands like /displace for a battle card or /demo-plan for a tailored demo script. These read from cached data — no re-fetching.

Inside a Brief

A brief is a single-page HTML document with every section backed by sourced data. Here's what each section contains:

TL;DR

3 bullets max. The 30-second executive summary. Read this before every call.

Account Snapshot

Company name, entity type, vertical classification, headquarters, size indicator (sites/students/employees), and federal funding profile. All sourced with footnote chips.

Pain Hypotheses

Ranked list of pain points mapped to Verkada product capabilities. Each hypothesis includes confidence level (high/medium/inference), evidence citations, and the specific Verkada product that addresses it.

Displacement Intel

Detected incumbent vendors (from job postings, RFPs, news). For each vendor, shows the Verkada displacement playbook from the persona file with specific counter-arguments.

Champions & Leadership

Named individuals with titles, MEDDIC role classifications (economic buyer, champion, coach), and champion fit scores. Sourced from LinkedIn, news mentions, and org charts.

MEDDIC Qualification

Full MEDDIC framework with interactive checkboxes. Each field (Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion) includes evidence, confidence, gap analysis, and source quality. See MEDDIC Workflow below.

GTM Strategy

Land play, POC strategy, bundle recommendation, procurement path (with contract numbers for SLED), expansion motion (3 phases), and competitive displacement playbook.

Discovery Questions by Persona

Questions organized by buyer persona (IT Director, Facilities, CSO, VP Ops, LP Director, Superintendent). Every question traces to a specific trigger that fired, with evidence and confidence. Includes inline leverage references (customer proof points).

Procurement Flowchart

SVG visual showing the recommended procurement path. Hover over each node for details. SLED accounts show cooperative purchasing vehicles; commercial accounts show direct/reseller paths.

Source Footnotes

Every claim in the brief has a colored footnote chip linking to the source URL. Green = primary source, yellow = secondary, red = inference. Click any chip to open the source.

All sections are collapsible — click any section header to collapse/expand. State is saved across page loads.

MEDDIC Discovery Workflow

Every brief includes a MEDDIC qualification section synthesized from cross-subagent data. Each field includes value, evidence array, confidence level, gap analysis, and source quality. The brief renders this as an interactive checklist.

How to use the MEDDIC section during discovery

1

Before the call: Review each MEDDIC field. The "gap" text tells you exactly what you need to confirm or discover. Use this to build your call agenda.

2

During the call: Use the discovery questions from the persona-matched section. Each question traces to a trigger, so you know why you're asking it.

3

After the call: Check off validated MEDDIC fields using the interactive checkboxes. The state persists across page loads so you can track progress across multiple calls.

The 6 MEDDIC fields

M
Metrics — Quantified business impact Verkada can deliver. Examples: consolidate 86 sites onto one platform, eliminate NVR maintenance across 42 buildings, comply with NDAA to protect $12M in federal funding.
E
Economic Buyer — The person who can say yes to the budget. Mapped from leadership data + persona role mappings. Named individuals when available; hypothesized from entity type when not.
D
Decision Criteria — What the buyer evaluates on. Derived from vertical drivers, pain hypotheses, and NDAA exposure. Examples: cloud-native architecture, single pane of glass, NDAA compliance.
D
Decision Process — How they buy. K-12 districts need board approval. Government entities use cooperative purchasing vehicles. Corporations have procurement departments. Derived from entity type + co-op data.
I
Identify Pain — Highest-confidence pain hypothesis that maps to a Verkada product capability. This is your opening angle in the conversation.
C
Champion — Named individual from champion signals enrichment with the highest champion fit score. The single highest-value MEDDIC field — this is who you need to find and win over.

Confidence badges: high = sourced from primary data, medium = inferred with evidence, inference = hypothesis needing validation.

Troubleshooting

Research hangs or never completes

Check the status page for stuck source clients. Most commonly caused by API rate limits (Google, SEC EDGAR). The pipeline has 120-second timeouts per client — stuck clients will eventually fail gracefully with insufficient_data.

Brief has many "insufficient_data" sections

This is normal for small, private companies with limited public presence. The platform refuses to invent data. Check which source clients returned empty — if key clients (news, SEC, LinkedIn) failed, it may be a transient API issue. Try re-running /research.

PDF looks wrong

The PDF uses your browser's native print dialog. For best results: choose "Save as PDF" as the destination, set margins to "Default", and enable "Background graphics" to preserve badge colors. Chrome/Edge produce the best output.

Chat says it doesn't have context

The chat panel loads brief data from the JSON file. If you're viewing a cached HTML file directly (not through the server), the chat won't have context. Access briefs through the homepage at http://localhost:8000.

No champion identified

Champion identification depends on leadership data from LinkedIn and news sources. Private companies and smaller organizations often have limited public leadership data. The brief will show this as a MEDDIC gap — use it as a discovery question.

Glossary tooltips not appearing on a term

The glossary uses case-insensitive matching with fallbacks for snake_case and humanized variants. If a term isn't matching, it may not be in data/glossary.yml. Tooltips only annotate the first occurrence per element to avoid visual clutter.

How AI Reasoning Works

Every AI-generated section in a brief is traceable through a 4-layer reasoning pipeline. Click "Show methodology" on any discovery question, pain hypothesis, champion card, or GTM recommendation to see exactly why it was generated.

The 4-Layer Pipeline

1
Triggers

Signals defined in persona/verkada-se.yml with detection rules and weights. Example: multi_site_sprawl, ndaa_compliance_pressure

2
Source Data

20+ source clients collect facts: NCES school data, SEC filings, job postings, news, cooperative purchasing, leadership profiles

3
Synthesizer

Cross-references source data against triggers. Fires triggers when evidence meets thresholds. Maps fired triggers to discovery templates

4
Customization

Template placeholders filled with account-specific facts. Leverage references matched by vertical. Generic templates become specific questions

Worked Example

Account: Fulton County Schools (86 schools, K-12 district)

  1. Trigger fires: multi_site_sprawl — detected because NCES data shows 86 school sites
  2. Source evidence: NCES reports 86 operational schools; news mentions "district-wide security upgrades"
  3. Template selected: "{company} manages {site_count} sites — how do you maintain visibility across all locations today?"
  4. Customization: Placeholder {company} → "Fulton County Schools", {site_count} → "86". Leverage reference added: (ref: Greenville County Schools — deployed 2,400 cameras across 106 sites)

What Each "Show Methodology" Panel Reveals

Discovery Q Trigger fired, trigger definition, source evidence that fired it, raw template from persona file, what was customized, leverage reference selected
Pain Mapped trigger, multi-source evidence trail, Verkada product match, confidence reasoning with source count
Champion 6-dimension scoring breakdown (Role Fit, Recency, Career Arc, Public Voice, Topic Affinity, Authority) with weights and individual scores
GTM Decision basis for land play, POC triggers, bundle selection logic, competitive displacement source, procurement path evidence
Products Pain hypotheses addressed by this product, matched triggers, selection logic from persona product lines

FAQ

Can I research any company?

Yes. The platform works across all Verkada ICP verticals: K-12, higher education, healthcare, retail, manufacturing, government, hospitality, senior living, transportation, critical infrastructure, and corporate. Public-sector and large commercial accounts produce the richest briefs because they have the most public data.

How long does a research run take?

Typically 2–4 minutes. Source collection runs in parallel (30–60 seconds), subagent analysis takes 30–45 seconds, and synthesis takes 45–90 seconds. The status page shows real-time progress.

Can I re-run research on the same account?

Yes. Re-running /research refreshes stale cached sources and generates a new brief with a new timestamp. Old briefs are preserved in briefs/.

What are the vertical example chips on the research bar?

Quick-access examples showing the types of accounts the platform handles. Click any chip to auto-fill the research bar with that example company name.

Why do some discovery questions have "(ref: ...)" at the end?

These are leverage references — real Verkada customer proof points from the persona file. They give you a specific customer story to cite during discovery. Example: "(ref: Greenville County Schools — deployed 2,400 cameras across 106 sites)".

What does "confidence: inference" mean?

The platform uses three confidence levels. High = backed by a primary source (SEC filing, official press release). Medium = inferred from secondary sources with evidence. Inference = hypothesis based on vertical patterns or weak signals. Always validate inference-level claims during discovery.

Do follow-up commands (like /displace) fetch new data?

No. Only /research fetches new data. All other commands (/displace, /demo-plan, /discovery-prep, /vertical-lens, /size, /followup) read from cached brief data. If the cache is missing, they fail with an error instead of fetching.

Can I use this offline?

Viewing previously generated briefs works offline (they're self-contained HTML files). Running new research requires internet access for source collection and API calls.
